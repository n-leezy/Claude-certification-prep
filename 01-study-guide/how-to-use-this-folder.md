# How to Use This Folder — The Learning Method

This kit is deliberately built around what the science of learning actually says works, not around re-reading and highlighting (which feel productive but barely move retention). Read this page once; it changes how you use everything else.

## The one idea that matters most

**Retrieval beats review.** Trying to *pull* an answer out of your head — even when you get it wrong — builds far more durable memory than reading the answer again. In a classic finding, students who studied a passage once and then *tested* themselves remembered ~50% more a week later than students who re-read the same passage repeatedly, despite spending less time with the material. This is the **testing effect** (a.k.a. retrieval practice or active recall).

Practical consequence: **the quizzes and flashcards in this kit are not the "check" at the end — they are the studying.** The domain notes exist to be the thing you retrieve, not the thing you marinate in.

## The six techniques baked into this kit

1. **Active recall / retrieval practice** — Self-quizzing instead of re-reading. *Where:* every `03-quizzes/` file, the `quiz.html` engine, and the "self-test" prompts inside each domain note. Close the notes, answer from memory, then check.

2. **Spaced repetition** — Reviewing material at expanding intervals (e.g., day 1, 3, 7, 16) rather than cramming. Spacing can improve long-term retention dramatically versus massed practice for the *same* total study time. *Where:* `flashcards.html` uses a **Leitner box** system (cards you miss come back fast; cards you know get spaced out), and the study plan schedules review days.

3. **Interleaving** — Mixing topics within a session instead of studying one block at a time. It feels harder and slower, but it sharpens your ability to tell similar concepts apart and improves recall. *Where:* the full practice exams mix all five domains; the plan tells you to interleave domains rather than finish one fully before starting the next.

4. **Elaboration & self-explanation** — Asking "why does this work? how does it connect to what I know?" *Where:* every quiz answer has a **"why"** explanation, and domain notes include "explain it to yourself" prompts.

5. **The Feynman technique** — Explain a concept in plain language as if teaching a beginner; the gaps where you stumble reveal what you don't actually understand. *Where:* each domain note ends with a "Teach-it-back" checklist.

6. **Deliberate practice & desirable difficulty** — Spend your time at the edge of your ability on the things you get wrong, not redoing what's already easy. Some friction during study (the "desirable difficulty" principle) is a feature, not a bug. *Where:* mark missed flashcards/quiz items and let the Leitner system resurface them.

## The daily loop (do this every study session)

```
1. WARM UP with retrieval (5 min)  → run yesterday's due flashcards before reading anything.
2. LEARN a small chunk (15–20 min) → read ONE section of a domain note, actively.
3. RETRIEVE immediately (10 min)   → close the note, do the matching quiz questions from memory.
4. ELABORATE (5 min)               → say out loud WHY each right answer is right (Feynman).
5. SPACE IT (2 min)                → add the chunk's cards to the flashcard deck for future days.
```

Short, frequent sessions beat long marathons. Two 30-minute sessions on different days are better than one 60-minute block.

## Flashcard quality rules (used to build this deck)

The included deck follows the **minimum-information principle**: each card asks for one small fact, worded so the "right bulb lights up" fast, with no "according to the text" framing. When you make your own cards, do the same — break big ideas into many tiny questions rather than one giant one.

## A note on honesty with yourself

Recall feels worse than re-reading because it's harder — that difficulty is exactly why it works. If a study method feels easy and fluent, be suspicious of it. Trust the struggle.
